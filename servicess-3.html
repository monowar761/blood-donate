Here's some information about red blood cells (RBCs) and their provision:

Blood types

Type O negative blood is considered a universal donor because it can be safely given to recipients with types A, B, or AB. Type AB positive is considered a universal recipient because it can safely receive the other ABO/Rh types.

Emergency provision

In an emergency, a blood sample should be collected as soon as possible before administering blood products. If the patient's blood type is not known, group O RhD negative uncrossmatched red cells may be issued for females of childbearing potential and pediatric males.

CMV-negative products

CMV-negative products can be provided upon request to reduce the risk of transfusion-transmitted CMV infection. This is especially important for high-risk patients such as pregnant CMV-negative women, fetuses receiving intrauterine transfusions, and neonates.

Transfusion decisions

The decision to transfuse should take into account the risks, benefits, and alternatives available. Factors to consider include cardiopulmonary reserve, volume of blood loss, and oxygen consumption.

Red blood cells, also known as erythrocytes, are microscopic, flat, disk-shaped cells that carry oxygen from the lungs to the body's tissues and transport carbon dioxide back to the lungs. They are continuously produced in the bone marrow and typically live for about 120 days.
