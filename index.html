Is Donating Blood Important, Why?

  • You can save multiple lives

    • Donating blood is not only an act of kindness but a way of paying it forward. There’s no telling when you or your loved one might need blood, and it’s good to know that the supply is there. By donating blood, you’re helping to ensure that blood is always available when needed.

  • Blood donations help patients recover faster

    • People who receive blood transfusions tend to recover faster than those who don’t. This is because the transfused blood provides the necessary nutrients and oxygen to the body, which helps it recover from an ailment or injury. A single blood donation can make a difference in someone's life and help them on the road to full recovery.

  • You might need blood one day

    • Giving blood can save multiple lives. That’s because your blood can be split into three components: red blood cells, plasma, and platelets. Each of these components can be separated and used to help different patients with various medical conditions. For instance, people with cancer require platelets to help them stop bleeding, while people who have undergone surgery may need red blood cells to replace the ones they have lost. Donating blood, therefore, helps in addressing the diverse medical needs of patients.

    • Blood is a body fluid in the circulatory system of humans and other vertebrates that delivers necessary substances such as nutrients and oxygen to the cells, and transports metabolic waste products away from those same cells. Blood is composed of blood cells suspended in blood plasma.

  • Classical Greek medicine

    • Robin Fåhræus (a Swedish physician who devised the erythrocyte sedimentation rate) suggested that the Ancient Greek system of humorism, wherein the body was thought to contain four distinct bodily fluids (associated with different temperaments), were based upon the observation of blood clotting in a transparent container. When blood is drawn in a glass container and left undisturbed for about an hour, four different layers can be seen. A dark clot forms at the bottom (the "black bile"). Above the clot is a layer of red blood cells (the "blood"). Above this is a whitish layer of white blood cells (the "phlegm"). The top layer is clear yellow serum (the "yellow bile").[42][failed verification] In general, Greek thinkers believed that blood was made from food.
